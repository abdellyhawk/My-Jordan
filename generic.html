Discover Jordan

Jordan, situated in the heart of the Middle East, serves as a bridge between continents, linking Asia, Africa, and Europe. The country is bordered by Saudi Arabia to the south and east, Iraq to the northeast, Syria to the north, and Palestine to the west. In the southern region, the majestic Wadi Rum desert, with its dramatic sandstone mountains, offers an otherworldly experience. Nearby, Petra, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, enchants visitors with its ancient rock-carved architecture.

These landscapes reflect the natural beauty and historical depth that define the country. Jordan is a land of contrasts and connections, where ancient heritage meets modern hospitality, making it a captivating destination for all travelers.

Tourist attraction

Petra

Known as the "Rose-Red City," Petra is an extraordinary ancient Nabatean city carved into rose-colored sandstone cliffs over 2,000 years ago. Hidden amidst the rugged mountains of southern Jordan, this archaeological marvel once served as a flourishing trading hub, strategically connecting the Silk Road, Arabia, and the Mediterranean. Its remarkable architecture, sophisticated water management system, and sheer scale have earned Petra its status as a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the New Seven Wonders of the World.

Visitors begin their journey through the Siq, a narrow, winding gorge flanked by towering cliffs, creating a sense of anticipation as sunlight dances on the sandstone walls. The Siq opens up dramatically to reveal the breathtaking Treasury (Al-Khazneh), one of Petra’s most iconic and best-preserved monuments, believed to have been a royal tomb or a temple. Beyond the Treasury, Petra unfolds into a sprawling archaeological site that tells the story of its vibrant past. Its wonders include elaborately carved royal tombs, majestic temples, and the ancient amphitheater, which could once seat over 6,000 spectators. Visitors can also explore the Street of Facades, a colonnaded street flanked by ornate tombs and shops, and the Great Temple, showcasing the city's grandeur at its peak.

For those seeking a challenge, a hike to the Monastery (Ad-Deir) is a must. Perched high atop a mountain, the Monastery rivals the Treasury in its scale and artistry, and the journey offers stunning views of the surrounding valleys. Along the way, visitors encounter Bedouin vendors selling handicrafts, jewelry, and refreshing tea, adding a touch of local culture to the adventure. Petra comes alive at night during the Petra by Night experience, where the Treasury is illuminated by thousands of candles, creating an ethereal atmosphere as traditional Bedouin music fills the air. This magical experience provides a deeper connection to the site's ancient spirit.

Whether marveling at its architectural genius, hiking its trails, or soaking in its timeless beauty, Petra is a destination that leaves an indelible impression. It is not just a testament to human ingenuity but also a place of wonder that continues to inspire awe in all who visit.

Wadi Rum

Also called the "Valley of the Moon," Wadi Rum is a dramatic desert landscape known for its red sand dunes, towering sandstone cliffs, and surreal rock formations. Located in southern Jordan, this UNESCO World Heritage Site is a natural wonder that has captivated adventurers and filmmakers alike. It has served as the backdrop for numerous films, including Lawrence of Arabia, The Martian, Dune, and Star Wars: The Rise of Skywalker, thanks to its otherworldly scenery.

Visitors can explore its beauty through various activities, such as thrilling jeep safaris that navigate its rugged terrain, camel treks that evoke ancient trade routes, and exhilarating hot-air balloon rides that provide a bird's-eye view of the sweeping desert. For the more adventurous, rock climbing on Wadi Rum’s massive sandstone cliffs offers a challenge with unparalleled views, while hiking trails reveal hidden canyons, ancient petroglyphs, and inscriptions etched by the Nabataeans thousands of years ago.

As night falls, Wadi Rum transforms into a stargazing paradise. Its remote location, far from urban light pollution, allows for an unforgettable celestial experience where countless stars, planets, and even the Milky Way become vividly visible. Staying in Bedouin camps adds a cultural dimension to the visit. These camps provide an authentic experience where travelers can enjoy the hospitality of the local Bedouin people. Guests are treated to traditional meals like zarb (a Bedouin-style barbecue cooked underground), listen to captivating tales passed down through generations, and experience traditional music and dance under the desert sky. Luxurious “glamping” options, complete with bubble tents and private amenities, cater to those seeking comfort without compromising on the immersive desert experience.

Wadi Rum is not only a haven for nature and culture enthusiasts but also a destination that inspires a deep connection to the Earth's timeless beauty and human history. From its striking landscapes to its rich cultural offerings, it remains a must-visit for those seeking adventure and tranquility alike.

Dead Sea

The Dead Sea, the lowest point on Earth at over 430 meters (1,412 feet) below sea level, is a natural wonder like no other. This hypersaline lake, located at the border between Jordan and Israel, is famous for its high salt concentration—ten times saltier than most seawater—making it impossible for most life to thrive. This unique characteristic allows visitors to float effortlessly on its surface, a truly surreal and relaxing experience.

The Dead Sea is not just about buoyancy; its waters are rich in minerals like magnesium, potassium, and bromine, renowned for their therapeutic and healing properties. These minerals are said to alleviate skin conditions such as psoriasis and eczema, as well as promote relaxation and well-being. Along its shores, the black mud is harvested for skincare treatments, often applied as masks or wraps to rejuvenate the skin and enhance circulation. Visitors can indulge in these treatments at the many luxurious resorts and spas dotting the coastline, providing a perfect retreat for wellness and relaxation.

The surrounding region also offers rich historical and cultural experiences. The ancient fortress of Masada, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, stands atop a plateau overlooking the Dead Sea. This site holds a dramatic history, as it was the last stronghold of Jewish rebels against the Romans. Visitors can hike or take a cable car to the summit to explore the ruins and enjoy breathtaking views of the Dead Sea and the Judean Desert. Another nearby attraction is Lot’s Cave, a biblical site tied to the story of Lot and his daughters, offering a fascinating glimpse into the region’s ancient spiritual significance. The Ein Gedi Nature Reserve, with its lush greenery, waterfalls, and hiking trails, is also a must-visit oasis that contrasts beautifully with the arid desert surroundings.

For those interested in science, the Dead Sea is a geological marvel, showcasing layers of salt and minerals that tell the story of its ancient history. As the lake continues to shrink due to environmental changes, the region has become a focus of ecological research and conservation efforts. Whether floating in its therapeutic waters, exploring its ancient sites, or simply marveling at its surreal beauty, the Dead Sea offers an unforgettable experience that combines relaxation, history, and natural wonder.

Aqaba

Aqaba, Jordan’s only coastal city, is a vibrant gateway to the Red Sea and a premier destination for both relaxation and adventure. Known for its crystal-clear waters, colorful coral reefs, and abundant marine life, Aqaba is a paradise for water enthusiasts. The warm, calm sea provides ideal conditions for snorkeling and scuba diving, offering unforgettable encounters with clownfish, lionfish, sea turtles, and even dolphins. Dive sites include stunning coral gardens, underwater pinnacles, and fascinating shipwrecks like the Cedar Pride, a sunken Lebanese freighter now home to thriving marine ecosystems.

For those who prefer to stay above water, Aqaba offers a variety of water sports, including kayaking, paddleboarding, and glass-bottom boat tours, which allow visitors to glimpse the vibrant underwater world without getting wet. Pristine beaches and luxury resorts line the coast, providing opportunities for sunbathing, relaxation, and world-class hospitality. Resorts often feature private beaches, infinity pools, and spa facilities, making Aqaba a perfect retreat. Beyond the water, Aqaba’s bustling souks and markets offer a taste of Jordanian culture and hospitality. Visitors can browse for unique local crafts, handmade jewelry, traditional spices, and textiles while enjoying the lively atmosphere. Don’t miss the opportunity to sample fresh seafood and authentic Jordanian cuisine at waterfront restaurants, where dishes are infused with Middle Eastern flavors.

Aqaba also boasts a rich history that adds depth to its seaside charm. The Aqaba Fort, a 14th-century Mamluk castle, stands as a testament to the city’s strategic importance throughout history. Nearby, the Ayla archaeological site reveals remnants of an early Islamic city, offering a glimpse into Aqaba’s historical significance as a vital trading post. The city’s connection to the Arab Revolt during World War I is also commemorated by the Arab Revolt Flagpole, one of the tallest flagpoles in the world.

For those looking to explore beyond Aqaba, its location makes it a convenient base for day trips to other iconic Jordanian attractions, such as Petra, Wadi Rum, and the Dead Sea. Whether you’re seeking underwater adventures, cultural exploration, or simple seaside relaxation, Aqaba promises an unforgettable blend of natural beauty, rich history, and warm hospitality.

Jerash

Often referred to as the "Pompeii of the East," Jerash is a spectacular testament to the grandeur of Roman civilization and one of the best-preserved ancient cities in the world. Located just north of Amman, this archaeological marvel invites visitors to step back in time and explore the awe-inspiring remnants of a once-thriving Roman metropolis.

Jerash is renowned for its remarkable architecture, featuring stunning colonnaded streets that stretch for miles and offer a glimpse into the city’s ancient splendor. Among its many highlights are the Temple of Zeus, perched atop a hill with breathtaking views, and the Temple of Artemis, dedicated to the city’s patron goddess, whose towering columns still stand as a testament to Roman engineering. The Oval Forum, a massive open plaza surrounded by graceful Ionic columns, is a striking centerpiece of the city and serves as a reminder of Jerash’s role as a social and political hub. Nearby, two well-preserved theaters—the South Theater and the smaller North Theater—showcase Roman ingenuity in acoustics and design. These venues once hosted performances, speeches, and debates, and today they continue to be used for cultural events and festivals. Visitors can also explore the remnants of ancient baths, elaborate fountains, and city gates, including the Hadrian’s Arch, built in honor of Emperor Hadrian’s visit in AD 129. The city’s well-preserved infrastructure, such as its stone-paved streets with visible chariot wheel marks, provides a tangible connection to the past.

Each summer, the Jerash Festival of Culture and Arts brings the ancient city to life. This vibrant celebration features traditional Jordanian music, folk dances, poetry readings, and theatrical performances, blending ancient heritage with modern cultural expression. The festival’s lively atmosphere offers a unique opportunity to experience Jerash as it might have been during its Roman heyday.

Whether marveling at the grandeur of its temples, strolling through its colonnaded avenues, or enjoying its modern cultural festivities, Jerash offers an unforgettable journey through time. It stands as a dynamic symbol of Jordan’s rich history and cultural legacy, captivating travelers with its seamless blend of ancient and modern allure.

Mount Nebo

Mount Nebo is a revered biblical site with profound historical and spiritual significance. According to tradition, it is the location where Moses stood to view the Promised Land before his death, as described in the Bible. Perched at an elevation of 817 meters (2,680 feet) above sea level, Mount Nebo offers breathtaking panoramic views of the Jordan Valley, the Dead Sea, and, on clear days, the distant hills of Jerusalem and Bethlehem. The serene and tranquil atmosphere of this sacred hilltop makes it a must-visit destination for pilgrims and history enthusiasts alike.

The site’s centerpiece is the Memorial Church of Moses, built to commemorate the prophet’s legacy. This modern basilica, constructed atop an ancient monastery, houses some of the finest examples of Byzantine mosaics in the region. These stunning mosaics, which date back to the 6th century, depict vivid scenes of nature, hunting, winemaking, and agriculture, offering a glimpse into the artistic and cultural heritage of the early Christian era. The church also features a bronze Serpentine Cross sculpture, symbolizing the biblical story of the bronze serpent raised by Moses in the wilderness. Visitors to Mount Nebo often take a moment to stand at the edge of the promontory, where Moses is believed to have gazed upon the land he could not enter. This vantage point provides a deeply moving experience, blending spirituality with awe-inspiring natural beauty. Nearby, travelers can explore the ancient town of Madaba, known as the City of Mosaics. Madaba is home to the famous Madaba Map, a meticulously crafted mosaic floor in the 6th-century Greek Orthodox Church of St. George. This map, the oldest known representation of the Holy Land, includes detailed depictions of Jerusalem and surrounding biblical sites, offering an invaluable link to history and scripture.

Mount Nebo and Madaba together form a significant stop on the King’s Highway, a historic route that has connected cultures and civilizations for centuries. Whether you’re drawn by faith, history, or the stunning landscapes, Mount Nebo offers a profound experience that lingers long after your visit, bridging the ancient past with the present.
